About

Background
I am a fifth year Ph.D. student in the Center for Robotics and Biosystems at Northwestern University, advised by Todd D. Murphey. I am part of the Interactive and Emergent Autonomy Lab, where our research focuses on computational methods in data-driven control, information theory in physical systems, and embodied intelligence.

I received my B.S. in Mechanical Engineering from the University of Notre Dame in 2016 and my M.S. in Mechanical Engineering from Northwestern University in 2018. I was also awarded a National Science Foundation (NSF) Graduate Research Fellowship in 2018. Please see my Projects and Publications pages for more, or check out my CV.

Research Interests
I am interested in how to optimally design robots -- by taking into account the relationship between hardware (including physical shape, sensors, and actuators), software (control policies, planning algorithms, and learning architecture), and the robot's goal. How should a robot's control strategy change depending on its morphology, and vice versa? Should it change its plan if a sensor breaks? Or should it adjust its morphology (e.g., lengthen a limb) if it learns something new about its environment?

In nature, biological organisms jointly evolve both their morphology and their neurological capabilities to improve their chances for survival. Consequently, task information is encoded both in their brains and their bodies. In robotics, the development of complex control and planning algorithms often bears sole responsibility for improving task performance. My work attempts to reduce the dependence on centralized control (especially for minimal systems and agents on the microscale, where there are computational limitations) for task execution by algorithmically designing and iterating on both a robot's controller and its physical body.

My projects approach this problem from different angles. In my Robot Comparisons project, I analyzed the information requirements for microrobots to achieve a goal with different combinations of sensors and actuators. Similarly, in the Collective Behaviors and Emergent Computation project my collaborators and I identified and exploited emergent behaviors in a system of minimal robots. We found that the collective actions the simple robots were physically capable of greatly reduced the necessary complexity of a controller. Next, in my Algorithmic Design of Minimal Robots work, I developed a methodology for algorithmically arranging sensing and actuation components into a robot design while maintaining a low level of design complexity, suitable for the constraints of the microrobots my collaborators at MIT were manufacturing. Those microrobots were also the motivation for my Bayesian Particles project, in which I modelled minimal agents (particles) navigating through the human circulatory system, collectively executing a Bayesian update. The agents work in parallel to collaboratively find a target and use only six bits of memory each. Lastly, my most recent project, Co-Design of a Tool and Strategy, brings this idea to the macroscale. I am enabling a robot to not only use a tool to assist it in accomplishing a task, but allowing the robot to design that tool by bending a flexible wire into a useful shape. These projects relate in their goals of offloading some of a robot's computational effort from a centralized controller onto the the robot's morphology, and taking advantage of existing structures and emergent properties native to the robotic system.
