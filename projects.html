Research

Artificial life is often thought of as complicated, expensive, large-scale implementations of humanoid robots, but the first life-like robotic behaviors may be the result of simple interactions collectively producing emergent behaviors.

Both the design of sensing, computation, and actuation components of individual robots, and predicting and understanding the collective interactions of groups of agents composed of these elements, are key to developing properties like self-organization and emergent computation.

Algorithmic Robot Design

Robots traditionally require three elements: sensors, computation, and actuators. How should robots be algorithmically designed using an existing library of these components?

Design Sensitivities. Robot performance is clearly sensitive to changes in design, and I am interested in determining how much a robot could benefit from the addition of different components or other morphologial changes.

For example, how does one determine the sensitivity of the design of a robotic leg (intended for walking) to the introduction of a knee? How does it affect the robot if the knee is a simple revolute joint, or if it has a motor? I am currently investigating this problem, both through the lens of reinforcement learning and using methods from switched systems theory.

Combinatoric Components. Another facet of robot design is the combinatorics of components. Sensors and actuators are by no means independent components on a robot. For example, a camera (which is a sensor) is useless at night without a flashlight (an actuator, of sorts). Likewise, a flashlight is of no benefit to a robot that doesn’t have a camera.

For control, exploitable sensor-actuator pairs are valuable. In my Master's Thesis, as well as a book chapter, I introduced a method for designing a simple robot when given a finite library of sensors and actuators.

Information Flow. A central idea in robot design is information flow from the environment to the robot. What is the relationship between the body of a robot and its desired task, without control? If the relative entropy between the initial conditions of the robot and the goal task is a small number (meaning that the goal task is not far from the initial conditions of the robot), then not much control information is necessary to achieve the task. In other words, if a task is well embodied by a robot, only a simple control policy is necessary to execute it. Otherwise more information, in the form of a complex control policy, is necessary.

Task embodiment is a measure of how much information a body encodes about a task. Changing a robot design can be thought of in terms of moving task information from the centralized computations in the control calculations to embedded computation in the physical body. This principle of information flow, and trade-offs of having control information be in the physical design of the body or in the computerized controller of the robot, are one way to formally think about robot designs. I introduced the concept of task embodiment in two publictions: a WAFR paper and a RA-L paper.

Collective Behaviors and Emergent Computation

I am interested in predicting the emergent properties of a collection of individual robots, and synthesizing these behaviors through the design of algorithmic and physical properties of each individual. The main element of emergent behavior is the ability of the local sensing and actuation of (potentially simple) individuals to result in global ensemble-level capabilities that are greater than what should be expected from such simple capabilities. How can a methodology be formed with which goals for the ensemble can be transformed into physical designs of individual robots?

Stochastic Robotics. First, emergent behaviors must be able to be identified and exploited. This was demonstrated in the [SR] paper, where directed locomotion was achieved through stochastic interactions. A nonparametric algorithm was used to take advantage of broken symmetries in the system and execute sophisticated collective behaviors, all with minimal sensing and actuation capabilities.

Reinforcement Learning in Particle Robots. Through collaboration with a chemical engineering lab at MIT, including Albert T. Liu, I have been considering the problem of designing microscopic robots (known as synthetic cells) to achieve a complex goal (e.g., mimicking the immune system by seeking invaders) in a complex environment (e.g., the circulatory system), where they might have to change their control policy, communicate with each other, and deal with uncertainty including false positives and negatives --- all with minimal capabilities and only a few bits of memory.

In a paper that has not yet been published, I demonstrate that synthetic cells can work together to find a target (or a moving target, or multiple targets) by performing a reinforcement learning-type alrogithm with which they update their control policy based on randomized envounters with other cells. As the synthetic cells work together to find the target(s) their interactions as an ensemble function as a physical implementation of a Bayesian update. That is, the particles act as a particle filter.
